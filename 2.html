SECTION A – Banking Services

1. Explain tangible and intangible banking services and their economic/monetary implications.
Banking provides tangible services like cheques, drafts, cards and ATMs, while intangible services include internet banking, customer support and advisory functions. These services promote financial inclusion, increase savings, support investment and influence credit creation. Their monetary impact includes money supply regulation, liquidity management and smoother economic transactions across the economy.
2. What are customer service deficiencies? How can banks improve them?
Customer service suffers due to delays, staff shortages, system failures, rude behavior and poor grievance handling. Banks can improve by using automation, training staff, offering faster digital services, reducing paperwork, tracking complaints, strengthening communication and adopting customer-centric policies to provide smooth, reliable and responsive service.
3. Briefly discuss economic and monetary implications of banking operations.
Banking operations influence savings, investments, credit availability and capital formation. They help regulate money supply, support monetary policy, maintain liquidity and stabilize the economy. Through lending, deposits and payment systems, banks directly affect inflation, interest rates, investment levels and overall economic growth.
4. Explain the goals of monetary policy in India.
Monetary policy aims to control inflation, maintain price stability, promote economic growth, ensure adequate credit, stabilize financial markets and manage exchange rates. Recent efforts focus on inflation targeting, liquidity management, interest rate adjustments and improving financial stability through RBI’s structured policy framework.
5. Discuss the impact of Monetary Policy on economic growth.
Monetary policy influences interest rates, credit availability and investment. Tight policy reduces inflation but slows growth, while expansionary policy boosts borrowing, consumption and investment. Proper balance helps maintain stability, support industry, control prices and encourage long-term economic development.
6. Discuss the importance of banking services in India.
Banking services promote financial inclusion, mobilize savings, support lending, enable digital transactions and contribute to economic development. They help government programs, encourage entrepreneurship, provide credit and strengthen economic stability by facilitating smooth financial operations nationwide.
7. What are Banking Services? Explain their importance.
Banking services include deposits, withdrawals, loans, digital payments, advisory support and remittances. They are important because they mobilize savings, support businesses, facilitate payments, enable investment and promote financial stability essential for economic growth.
8. Write a note on Deficiency in Services and ways to Improve it.
Service deficiencies arise from delays, rude behavior, technical issues and poor communication. Improvement requires staff training, advanced technology, faster grievance handling, customer-friendly policies and continuous monitoring to enhance service quality.
9. Do banks provide services other than deposits and loans?
Yes, banks offer advisory services, credit cards, fund transfers, lockers, digital banking, insurance distribution, remittance services and investment products. These diversified services improve convenience and support customer needs.
10. Different ways banks can provide better customer services.
Banks can improve service by using digital tools, reducing queue times, offering better communication, providing personalized banking, strengthening staff training, improving grievance handling and ensuring faster, secure transactions.

SECTION B – Lending Services

1. What are loans and advances? Explain principles of sound lending.
Loans and advances are funds provided by banks to customers. Sound lending principles include safety, liquidity, profitability, purpose, security, diversification and creditworthiness. Banks must evaluate risk, borrower capacity and repayment ability before lending.
2. Forms of loans including general loans, overdrafts, term advances.
General loans are fixed-repayment borrowings. Overdrafts allow withdrawal beyond balance. Term advances are medium or long-term loans for assets or business needs. These forms support personal, industrial and commercial financial requirements.
3. Discuss Forms of Advances.
Forms include overdrafts, cash credit, term loans, consumer loans, bills discounting, hire-purchase advances, packing credit, agricultural loans and industrial financing. Each form meets specific financial needs of individuals and businesses.
4. Note on industrial advances and small borrower advances.
Industrial advances finance machinery, expansion and modernization. Advances to small borrowers support small businesses, artisans and traders through simplified procedures, low collateral and priority lending schemes to promote inclusive growth.
5. Define Bill of Lading.
A Bill of Lading is a legal transport document issued by a carrier to a shipper acknowledging goods receipt. It serves as proof of shipment, evidence of contract and document of title used in trade finance.
6. Institutions granting agricultural loans.
Key institutions include NABARD, Regional Rural Banks, Cooperative Banks, Commercial Banks and Microfinance institutions. Their objectives include supporting farmers, improving rural credit, providing crop and term loans and promoting agricultural development.
7. Notes on Crop Loans, Term Loans, EXIM Bank.
Crop loans meet seasonal farming needs. Term loans finance equipment and long-term activities. EXIM Bank promotes export-import trade by offering credit, guarantees and support to Indian businesses trading globally.
8. Role of banks in financing foreign trade.
Banks provide letters of credit, bill discounting, foreign exchange services, remittance facilities and export financing. They reduce risk, ensure payment security and support international trade operations effectively.
9. Lending services and foreign bill purchase.
Lending services include loans, advances and overdrafts. Foreign bill purchase occurs when a bank buys a bill drawn on foreign buyers, providing quick funds to exporters before actual payment arrives.
10. Note on agricultural financing.
Agricultural financing includes crop loans, irrigation loans, equipment financing and rural development credit. It ensures timely funds for farmers, improving productivity, income and rural economic growth.

SECTION C – Regulations for Banking

1. Definition of banking under Banking Regulation Act 1949.
The Act defines banking as accepting deposits for lending or investment. Banks may also engage in foreign exchange, remittances, trade finance and agency services. RBI controls licensing, management, audits and operations to ensure financial stability.
2. Conditions for an instrument to be a cheque.
A cheque must be a written, unconditional order, signed by the drawer, directing a bank to pay a specific sum to a person or bearer. It must be payable on demand and drawn on a specific bank.
3. Discuss Bills of Exchange and Promissory Notes.
A bill of exchange is an unconditional written order directing payment. A promissory note is a written promise to pay. Both are negotiable instruments used for credit, trade and settlements.
4. Regulatory framework governing banks.
Bank regulation involves RBI Act, Banking Regulation Act, Negotiable Instruments Act and government policies. These ensure stability, protect depositors, regulate credit, control risks and maintain financial discipline across banking operations.
5. What are Negotiable Instruments?
Negotiable instruments are transferable documents guaranteeing payment, including cheques, bills of exchange and promissory notes. They ensure safe, efficient financial transactions and facilitate trade settlements.
6. Explain RBI Act 1934.
The RBI Act established the Reserve Bank of India as the central bank responsible for currency issuance, monetary policy, financial regulation, banking supervision and maintaining economic stability across the country.
7. Relationship between banker and customer.
The banker-customer relationship includes debtor-creditor, trustee-beneficiary, agent-principal and advisor-client roles. It is based on trust, confidentiality, service delivery and compliance with legal and contractual obligations.
8. Meaning of holder and holder in due course.
A holder possesses a negotiable instrument legally. A holder in due course obtains it for value, in good faith, before maturity. They enjoy stronger rights, including free-from-defect claims.

SECTION D – E-Banking & Reforms

1. Define E-banking with advantages and obstacles.
E-banking provides digital banking through internet, ATM, mobile apps and cards. Advantages include speed, convenience and 24/7 access. Obstacles include security issues, technical failures, cyber risks and digital illiteracy.
2. Determinants of service quality – SERVQUAL.
SERVQUAL includes reliability, responsiveness, assurance, empathy and tangibles. These dimensions measure service gaps, evaluate customer expectations and help banks improve satisfaction and performance.
3. BASEL II Accord and importance.
Basel II sets global banking standards on capital adequacy, risk management and supervisory review. It improves financial stability by ensuring banks maintain sufficient capital and manage operational, credit and market risks properly.
4. Asset classification.
Assets are classified as standard, sub-standard, doubtful and loss assets. Guidelines ensure proper provisioning, identify non-performing assets and maintain banking stability through disciplined credit management.
5. Internet banking merits and demerits.
Internet banking offers convenience, lower cost, instant access and wide services. Demerits include security risks, fraud, dependency on internet and technical issues affecting service reliability for some users.
6. Implementation of banking sector reforms.
Reforms include deregulation, improved supervision, NPA control, digital adoption and capital strengthening. They aim to enhance efficiency, transparency, profitability and global competitiveness in Indian banking.
7. Globalised challenges in banking.
Challenges include competition, cyber threats, compliance requirements, technology upgrades, capital needs and customer expectations. Banks must innovate and strengthen risk management to stay competitive.
8. Capital adequacy and its adoption.
Capital adequacy means maintaining minimum capital to absorb losses. India follows Basel norms through RBI, ensuring stable, strong banking systems capable of managing financial risks.
9. Elements of service quality.
Service quality includes reliability, speed, transparency, empathy, security, communication and professionalism. These factors improve customer satisfaction and long-term trust.
10. Notes on debit card, credit card, mobile banking, smart cards.
Debit cards deduct money instantly; credit cards allow borrowing; mobile banking provides phone-based services; smart cards store data for secure payments. These tools improve convenience and digital transactions.
11. Notes on ATM and capital adequacy ratio.
ATMs provide cash withdrawal and other services anytime. Capital adequacy ratio measures a bank’s capital relative to risks, ensuring financial strength and safety.
12. Short challenges in banking sector.
Challenges include cybercrime, rising NPAs, competition, regulatory compliance, digital transformation costs and customer demands. Banks must innovate and manage risks effectively.
13. Difference between debit and credit cards.
Debit cards use your own bank balance, while credit cards allow borrowed spending up to a limit. Debit avoids debt; credit offers rewards but requires repayment.

Other Important Questions

1. Money supply measures and rationale.
Money supply includes M1, M2, M3 and M4. Each measure captures liquidity levels in the economy. They help policymakers analyze spending, inflation, financial stability and the effectiveness of monetary policy.
2. Difference between repo and reverse repo.
Repo rate is interest at which RBI lends to banks; reverse repo is interest at which RBI borrows from banks. Repo controls liquidity injection, reverse repo absorbs liquidity.
3. Define Variable Reserve Ratio.
Variable Reserve Ratio refers to RBI adjusting CRR and SLR requirements to control credit, liquidity and inflation. It influences banks’ lending capacity.
4. Flexible Deposits.
Flexible deposits combine savings and fixed deposit features, allowing partial withdrawals while earning higher interest. They provide liquidity and better returns.
5. Simple guarantee.
A simple guarantee is a contract where the guarantor promises to repay a debt if the principal borrower defaults. It applies to a single transaction.
6. Tier I Capital.
Tier I capital includes core equity, reserves and retained earnings. It represents the strongest form of bank capital used to absorb losses.
7. Loss Assets.
Loss assets are NPAs considered uncollectible and require full provisioning. They weaken bank profitability and stability.
8. Endorsement of Negotiable Instrument.
Endorsement is signing a negotiable instrument to transfer ownership. It allows free transfer and facilitates secure settlement in trade transactions.
